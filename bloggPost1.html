Starting the Journey

This is the first post in a series where I’ll dive into UX and accessibility; two topics that, once you really understand them, change how you see the digital world.

Back in university, I had a lecturer who left a mark on me. He wasn’t teaching accessibility from a distance; As a kid, he had been bound to a wheelchair, and later he worked on all kinds of initiatives;from collaborating with disability groups to helping homeless individuals build systems and habits to get back on their feet. He was essentially doing real-world behavior design long before I even knew what that meant.

In his course I learned about visual and hearing impairments, but also the less visible ones; ADHD, Asperger’s, and other neurodivergences that fundamentally shift how someone experiences the web. Suddenly, “good UX” wasn’t just about clean interfaces. It was about creating environments where more people can succeed with less friction.

One day he took us to an office staffed largely by people with disabilities. Watching them work was like discovering hidden layers in tools we all take for granted. For example, watching a blind user type on an iPhone using braille input:

I also learned that every feature in Facebook is accessible via keyboard alone—useful not only for blind users but for anyone with limited vision or motor challenges. These small considerations compound into real-world impact.

Why Accessibility Matters More Than Ever

Today, accessibility isn’t just a nice-to-have; it’s increasingly a legal requirement. Initiatives like WCAG and the broader A11y movement (pronounced “A-one-one-Y”) provide frameworks that help us design in a way that includes more people instead of excluding them.

Because I want to actively contribute to this space in my work, I’m exploring these frameworks, test the tools, and share the practical bits; so you can improve your own code and process too. Even if only a few people read this blog, it will serve as a place for me to point to when other's say “Where should I begin?”

A Talk Worth Starting With

To kick things off, I’m using a fantastic conference talk from NordicJS. It’s approachable but packed with the kind of insights that make you pause the video to take notes. The speaker lays out several foundational concepts and tools I’ll be exploring throughout this series.

What Is Accessibility?

“Accessibility is the quality of being easily reached, entered, or used by people who have a disability.”

That’s the core. Everything else—guidelines, standards, best practices—are simply structured ways of getting there.

And accessibility isn’t just for websites. As the talk points out, it applies to everything around the web: tools, technologies, interfaces, mobile devices, smartwatches, and whatever we invent next.

“It’s not just the web. It’s the tools around it;and the expectation that anyone with a disability can use them… via any device we have now or invent in the future.”

Accessibility Helps All of Us

Here’s where it gets interesting: accessibility isn’t just for “other people.” You and I benefit from it constantly.

  • Old phone
  • Broken arm
  • Eye infection
  • Noisy environment
  • Bright sunlight
  • Slow internet or limited bandwidth

If the design works in these edge cases, it almost certainly improves the experience for the average user. This is why designing for the extremes is one of the most powerful concepts in product development.

And if you’re in the EU, accessibility is now encouraged—and in many cases enforced—by law.

WCAG: The Framework Behind It All

WCAG—the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines—is the backbone of global accessibility standards. It defines three levels of accessibility and provides a structured way to evaluate your site.

This topic deserves its own deep-dive post, so I’ll save the full breakdown for later.
